Move-Out Deduction Dispute Law in Florida

Florida Statutes § 83.49 controls how residential landlords must handle security deposits and move-out deductions. After you vacate, the landlord has two clear options. If the landlord does not intend to keep any of the deposit, they must return the full amount within 15 days after you move out. If the landlord intends to keep all or part of the deposit, they must send you written notice by certified mail to your last known address within 30 days after you vacate. That notice must state the landlord's intent to impose a claim and the specific reason for keeping the money. The statute even provides specific language the landlord must substantially follow.

If the landlord fails to send the proper written notice within 30 days, the landlord forfeits the right to make any claim on the deposit and must return it in full. This is a strict deadline, and Florida courts enforce it. If the landlord does send proper notice, you have 15 days from receipt to object in writing. If you do not object, the landlord can deduct the claimed amount and must send you any remainder within 30 days after the objection period ends.

Deductions are limited to actual damages beyond normal wear and tear, unpaid rent, or other amounts specifically allowed by the lease. Normal wear and tear, such as minor carpet wear, faded paint, or small nail holes, cannot legally be deducted. Charges for routine cleaning, repainting between tenants, or pre-existing damage are commonly disputed and frequently improper. Under § 83.49(3)(c), the prevailing party in any deposit dispute is entitled to recover reasonable attorney's fees and court costs, which dramatically shifts leverage in favor of tenants with valid claims.

How a Demand Letter Works in Florida

A Florida move-out deduction dispute letter works because it puts the landlord on notice that you know the law and are prepared to enforce it. The letter should be sent in writing, ideally by certified mail with return receipt, and should arrive within the 15-day objection window after you receive the landlord's notice of intent to impose a claim. Preserving that deadline is critical to keeping all your rights.

An effective letter does several things at once. It identifies the rental, the move-out date, and the deposit amount. It cites Florida Statutes § 83.49 directly and references any procedural failures, such as a late notice, missing certified mail, or vague reasons for the deduction. It then itemizes each disputed charge and explains why it is improper, distinguishing normal wear and tear from actual damage and pointing out any charges that exceed reasonable repair costs.

The letter should include a clear demand for the return of a specific dollar amount within a reasonable deadline, often 10 to 14 days. It should also remind the landlord of the attorney's fee provision in § 83.49(3)(c), which means the landlord risks paying your legal fees if you sue and win. Many landlords, especially larger property managers, will refund disputed amounts rather than litigate a case where the statute and fee-shifting clearly favor the tenant. Attaching photos, the move-in checklist, and copies of communications strengthens the letter. Even when it does not produce an immediate refund, a properly documented demand letter becomes powerful evidence in small claims court.

Procedural Notes for Florida

If the landlord does not respond, you can file in Florida small claims court, which handles disputes up to $8,000 under Florida Small Claims Rule 7.010. Filing fees typically range from about $55 to $300 depending on the claim amount and county. You generally must file in the county where the rental is located or where the landlord resides. Florida's statute of limitations for written contracts is five years, and four years for oral contracts, under Fla. Stat. § 95.11, so you have ample time to sue, but acting promptly preserves evidence. Remember that § 83.49 allows the prevailing party to recover attorney's fees, which can make hiring a lawyer financially viable even for modest deposit claims.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does my Florida landlord have to return my security deposit?
Under Florida Statutes § 83.49, your landlord must return the full deposit within 15 days after you move out if they are not making any claim against it. If the landlord intends to keep all or part of the deposit, they must send you written notice by certified mail within 30 days after you vacate, stating the reason for the claim. Missing the 30-day notice deadline forfeits the landlord's right to keep any portion of the deposit.
What counts as normal wear and tear in Florida?
Florida law does not provide a precise definition, but courts generally treat minor carpet wear, faded or scuffed paint, small nail holes, worn door hinges, and gradual aging as normal wear and tear that cannot be deducted. Actual damage like large holes, pet stains, broken fixtures, or excessive filth can be charged. Routine cleaning and repainting between tenants are usually not chargeable unless the unit was left in unusually poor condition beyond ordinary use.
What happens if I do not object within 15 days of the landlord's notice?
If you fail to send a written objection within 15 days after receiving the landlord's notice of intent to impose a claim, the landlord may legally deduct the amount stated in the notice. You can still potentially challenge improper deductions in court, but missing the 15-day window significantly weakens your position. Always send your dispute letter by certified mail within the deadline to fully preserve your statutory rights under § 83.49.
Can I recover attorney's fees if I sue my Florida landlord?
Yes. Florida Statutes § 83.49(3)(c) states that in any action brought to enforce the security deposit statute, the prevailing party is entitled to receive reasonable attorney's fees and court costs from the losing party. This fee-shifting provision is a powerful tool for tenants. It encourages landlords to settle valid disputes and makes it economically realistic to hire an attorney for relatively small deposit cases that would otherwise not justify legal representation.
Do I need a lawyer to file in Florida small claims court?
No, Florida small claims court is designed for self-represented parties and handles disputes up to $8,000. Procedures are simplified, and judges are accustomed to working with non-lawyers. You file a Statement of Claim in the county where the property is located, pay a filing fee that varies by claim size, and present your evidence at a pretrial conference. Bring your lease, move-in and move-out photos, the landlord's notice, and your dispute letter.
